Methodology

A four-phase approach to modeling, managing, and publishing born-digital archival materials through semantic web technologies

The Valerio Evangelisti Archive project implements a comprehensive methodological framework structured across four principal phases. Each phase addresses specific challenges in the representation, modeling, management, and dissemination of born-digital authorial materials, responding to the complexity inherent in contemporary digital documentation through the adoption of Linked Open Data technologies and semantic web standards.

01

Data Analysis
& Requirements

The preliminary research phase involved phenomenological analysis of born-digital authorial materials and identification of specific challenges in their representation. Through the convergence of state-of-the-art review, material analysis, and methodological reflection, five fundamental requirements were identified for modeling, considering their identity, structure, provenance, and contexts.

The Evangelisti Dataset

The requirements emerged from direct engagement with the born-digital partition of the Valerio Evangelisti Archive, which documents the author's digital activity from the early 1990s through 2022. As a pioneer in computer adoption since the 1980s, Evangelisti consistently experimented with new technologies, integrating them into his creative and documentary practices.

The collection analyzed comprises:

  • Desktop computer hard drive: complete contents documenting primary production environment
  • External hard drive: backup and supplementary materials
  • 93 floppy disks: early digital materials (out of 389 total in the collection)

This material diversity—ranging from word processing documents and research notes to email archives, multimedia files, and system backups—exemplifies the representational challenges that contemporary born-digital archives must address. The stratification of formats, software environments, and organizational logics present in the Evangelisti materials directly informed the articulation of the modeling requirements.

Five Modeling Requirements

From the analysis of the Evangelisti Archive's complexity, five fundamental requirements emerged for representing born-digital authorial materials:

01

File Representation

The model must enable explicit representation of a file's nature, distinguishing between physical, logical, and content dimensions, supporting typological diversity without privileging or excluding specific formats.

02

Material Integrity

The model must support the representation of hash codes and the algorithms used to calculate them (MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256, SHA-512), as identifying elements of the document and its integrity over time.

03

Native Metadata

The model must ensure systematic integration of native metadata, documenting their value and provenance, without a priori constraints on the types or granularity of information collected.

04

Provenance

The model must support representation of technical, procedural, and interpretive provenance, documenting the history and transformations of digital documents and their related metadata.

05

Contexts

The model must enable the representation of multiple contexts, allowing documents to be connected to the manifold relationships, vertical and horizontal, that enrich their meaning, ensuring articulated and contextualized understanding.

These five requirements collectively aim to generate semantic graphs enabling movement toward an "augmented inventory": an informational ecosystem that expands the traditional boundaries of the archive to amplify interpretive contexts and access possibilities.

02

Ontological
Model: BoDi

In response to the identified requirements, BoDi (Born-Digital Ontology) was developed as a specialized extension of RiC-O dedicated to representing born-digital authorial materials.

Choice of RiC-O as Base Framework

RiC-O was selected as the reference framework for its capacity to represent complex, multidirectional networks of relationships among archival entities, agents, and contexts, reflecting the stratified and interconnected nature of contemporary documentation. RiC-O is progressively establishing itself as a new international reference standard, developed by the ICA through an extensive and participatory process. EGAD documentation recognizes that "RiC is designed with the expectation that it may be extended using cross-domain ontologies specifically addressing technical data needed for the management of instantiations."

Model Architecture

BoDi selectively integrates classes and properties from established ontologies:

  • RiC-O: utilized in its entirety as reference framework
  • PREMIS: for preservation metadata and integrity
  • PROV-O: for procedural provenance
  • LRMoo: for integration with the bibliographic domain

These are complemented by classes and properties developed ad hoc to address specific needs of born-digital authorial materials not covered by existing models.

Modeling Requirement 1: File Stratification

Content Level

Modeled through rico:RecordResource and its specializations (rico:Record, rico:RecordSet, rico:RecordPart).

Logical Level

Represented by rico:Instantiation, which identifies the file as a logical entity distinct from content, correlated via rico:isOrWasInstantiationOf and rico:hasOrHasInstantiation.

BoDi enriches rico:Instantiation with specific properties:

  • bodi:hierarchyDepth: measures file depth in folder hierarchy
  • prov:atLocation: documents the file path through prov:Location
  • bodi:hasFormatNote / bodi:isTechnicalDescriptionOf: link technical descriptions with types distinguishing human and AI-generated productions

Physical Level

Expressed by the bodi:FileClusterPosition class, which describes the physical allocation of bits on the medium, correlated to the logical level via rico:hasFileClusterPosition and rico:isFileClusterPositionOf.

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: The manuscript file "Tortuga.docx" exemplifies this tripartite stratification. At the content level, it represents the intellectual work—a novel by Evangelisti—modeled as a rico:Record with its title and authorship. At the logical level, the instantiation captures its existence as a DOCX file located at depth 2 within the folder hierarchy ("/Desktop/Romanzi/Tortuga.docx"). At the physical level, the file's bit sequences are documented through their storage location on Evangelisti's external hard drive. This multi-layered representation enables queries spanning from "all works by Evangelisti" to "all files stored on specific hardware" without conflating these distinct dimensions.

Modeling Requirement 2: Integrity and Hash

Integrity is modeled through integration of the premis:Fixity class, correlated to rico:Instantiation via bodi:hasHashCode and bodi:isHashCodeOf. The symmetric property bodi:hasSameHashCodeAs enables explicit documentation of relationships between instantiations sharing the same hash value, supporting automatic duplicate identification and migration operation verification.

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: The file "Tortuga.docx" has a SHA-256 hash value of "a3d5c8f2e9b1a7c4d6e8f0a2b4c6d8e0f2a4b6c8d0e2f4a6b8c0d2e4f6a8b0c2", calculated through a documented rico:Activity performed on January 15, 2025, using the SHA-256 algorithm. This same hash value was also found in a backup copy of the file, enabling the system to automatically identify them as duplicates through the bodi:hasSameHashCodeAs property. The complete provenance chain—including the algorithm used, the date of calculation, and the software stack employed—is preserved within the knowledge graph, ensuring long-term verifiability of file integrity even as preservation environments evolve.

Modeling Requirement 3: Native Metadata

BoDi proposes a flexible and agnostic approach through:

  • bodi:TechnicalMetadata: linked to rico:Instantiation via bodi:hasTechnicalMetadata, represents metadata value through rdf:value
  • bodi:TechnicalMetadataType: represents metadata type (e.g., FileSize, Software, cp:revision)
  • bodi:TechnicalMetadataTypeSet: enables grouping of different types according to functional criteria

The modeling preserves values and types in the exact form retrieved by the extraction tool, without normalization. Extraction is modeled as rico:Activity, permitting coexistence of metadata from different extractions as separate instances.

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: From "Tortuga.docx", Apache Tika extracted numerous native metadata including the revision count (17 revisions, labeled "cp:revision") and file size (286 KB, labeled "FileSize"). Each metadata value exists as a distinct bodi:TechnicalMetadata instance linked to its bodi:TechnicalMetadataType. The extraction process itself is documented as a rico:Activity performed on January 15, 2025, supervised by archivist Lucia Giagnolini, using a bodi:SoftwareStack comprising Apache Tika 2.9.1, Python 3.11.5, and the Apache POI parser for Microsoft Office documents. This granular documentation enables comparative analysis: if the same file were later extracted using ExifTool, the resulting metadata would coexist as separate instances, each traceable to its specific extraction context, permitting quality assessment across different tools.

Modeling Requirement 4: Provenance

BoDi articulates provenance through three dimensions:

Custodial History

Realized through PREMIS classes premis:StorageMedium and premis:StorageLocation. Chain of custody is documented through rico:DerivationRelation.

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: The external hard drive materials underwent three documented migrations, each represented as distinct instantiations linked through derivation relationships. The original instantiation resided on a Western Digital My Book 2TB USB 3.0 drive at the Associazione Valerio Evangelisti headquarters. A first migration transferred the materials to a Samsung Portable SSD T7 1TB stored in Cabinet 1 at ADLab (University of Bologna, Via Zamboni 32). A subsequent migration moved them to a Network Attached Storage (Ext4) in ADLab's Server Room. Each storage location and medium is explicitly documented, with rico:DerivationRelation instances tracking the source-to-target lineage. This comprehensive documentation enables researchers to verify the materials' custodial history and assess any potential implications of hardware changes on file integrity.

Agency

BoDi extends the provenance principle by including algorithmic agency alongside human agency:

  • Human agency: through RiC-O properties and bodi:generatedBy for computational process outputs
  • Algorithmic agency: through rico:Mechanism subclasses: bodi:Software, bodi:Algorithm, bodi:SoftwareComponent; and bodi:SoftwareStack for composite aggregations
  • Hybrid agency: through bodi:isOrWasSupervisorOf to document human governance over digital processes, and bodi:hasRevisioned/bodi:revisedBy for validated AI-generated content

The properties bodi:confidenceScore and bodi:hasHumanValidation document quality and reliability of AI-generated outputs.

Processes

Every significant operation is modeled as rico:Activity. The bodi:Exception class represents error conditions or anomalous situations. The bodi:omittedInformation property documents anonymization interventions for privacy reasons.

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: The metadata extraction process for "Tortuga.docx" exemplifies the representation of hybrid agency. The rico:Activity of extraction involved a bodi:SoftwareStack (Apache Tika, Python, and specialized parsers) performing the computational work, while archivist Lucia Giagnolini exercised governance through the bodi:isOrWasSupervisorOf relationship. When AI-generated technical descriptions were produced for files, the bodi:hasRevisioned property documents which descriptions underwent human validation, with bodi:confidenceScore values enabling prioritization of review efforts. Privacy-sensitive materials are marked with bodi:omittedInformation, transparently documenting curatorial decisions to redact information while preserving the structural integrity of the archive for quantitative analysis.

Modeling Requirement 5: Contexts

Vertical Dimension

BoDi fully adopts the RiC-O framework, representing hierarchy through rico:Record and rico:RecordSet, linked by rico:includesOrIncluded and rico:isOrWasIncludedIn. RiC-O's agnosticism regarding canonical descriptive levels enables representation of digital organizations without forcing, with possibility of selective typing via rico:RecordSetType.

Horizontal Dimension

Realized through integration with LRMoo. The lrmoo:F1_Work class represents the literary work as a conceptual entity, distinguished from its material manifestations, linked to rico:RecordResource via rico:isOrWasSubjectOf or rico:isRelatedTo. lrmoo:F2_Expression enables modeling of specific realizations of the work (editions, translations, revisions).

Example from the Evangelisti Archive: The "Tortuga.docx" file demonstrates both contextual dimensions. Vertically, it is nested within the folder hierarchy: the file (instantiation) is contained within the "Romanzi" folder (a rico:RecordSet), which in turn is part of the Desktop directory structure. At the content level, the record representing Tortuga is included within the broader recordset of Evangelisti's novels. Horizontally, the file connects to the literary work "Tortuga" (modeled as lrmoo:F1_Work), enabling discovery of all archival materials related to this novel regardless of their hierarchical location—manuscripts, correspondence with publishers, reviews, translations. A researcher interested in Tortuga can thus navigate from the published work to find drafts, notes, and revisions scattered across different folders, reconstructing the creative genealogy of the text through semantic relationships rather than relying solely on file system organization.

Data Population and Workflow Automation

The BoDi model was populated with data from the born-digital materials of the Evangelisti Archive through a structured Python pipeline that enabled automation of the data generation process. This computational workflow systematically processed the archival materials, extracting technical metadata, calculating hash codes, documenting provenance information, and generating RDF triples conforming to the BoDi ontological specifications.

The automated pipeline ensures:

  • Consistency in metadata extraction and representation across the entire collection
  • Scalability for processing large volumes of heterogeneous digital materials
  • Reproducibility of the data generation process for quality control and verification
  • Documentation of all computational processes as rico:Activity instances within the knowledge graph

The pipeline architecture modularly integrates various software tools and libraries, with each component's role and configuration explicitly documented within the resulting RDF graph, thereby implementing the provenance principles articulated in Requirement 4.

The BoDi model enables research paths according to multiple logics: hierarchical, thematic, chronological, typological, relational, procedural, and contextual/transversal through combinations of multiple criteria.

03

Data Management:
Blazegraph

For managing and querying data represented in BoDi, Blazegraph was adopted as the reference triplestore. The selection of this technological environment responded to the necessity of identifying a platform capable of enabling publication, exploration, and querying of descriptive data in RDF format, while ensuring separation between data structure and presentation modality.

Key Capabilities

  • Efficient storage of large-scale RDF graphs
  • Querying through complex SPARQL queries
  • Native integration with visualization platforms based on Semantic Web technologies
  • Scalability necessary for managing articulated knowledge graphs such as the Evangelisti Archive

SPARQL Endpoint

The SPARQL endpoint enables researchers and advanced users to execute personalized queries on the data, retrieving information according to specific criteria and combinations of parameters not predefined in the graphical interface. This architecture ensures separation between the storage/querying level and the presentation level, permitting multiple access modalities to the same informational base.

Standard management of data through the SPARQL endpoint and their visualization through a dedicated interface represented essential technical requirements for selecting the adoption environment.

04

Data Presentation:
ResearchSpace

For publication, exploration, and data access, ResearchSpace was selected as the platform—an open-source environment for managing and publishing knowledge graphs dedicated to cultural heritage.

Platform Characteristics

ResearchSpace, developed at the British Museum with support from the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and currently maintained by Kartography CIC, is defined by its developers as a platform designed to "help establish a community of researchers, where their underlying activities are framed by data sharing, active engagement in formal arguments, and semantic publishing."

The architecture is based on Semantic Web technologies, utilizing RDF for data representation and SPARQL as query language. The system is structured in integrable and configurable modules (templates), each offering specialized functionalities adaptable to individual project needs.

Available Modules

  • Semantic Search and Forms: contextual and faceted searches
  • Knowledge Patterns: reusable ontological schemas for adding and modifying complex relationships
  • Knowledge Maps: network visualization of connections between actors, places, events, objects, and concepts
  • Image Viewer: comparison and annotation of high-resolution images
  • Semantic Narratives: combination of text and dynamic visualizations
  • Timelines and geographic maps: for temporal and spatial data visualization

Implementation for the Evangelisti Archive

The project represented the first implementation of ResearchSpace based on an archival ontology (BoDi/RiC-O), verifying its compatibility with frameworks different from CIDOC CRM, for which the system was originally designed.

Semantic Tree Advanced

To address the specific needs of the Evangelisti Archive, an extended version of the Semantic Tree module was developed, introducing:

  • Progressive loading: Instead of retrieving the entire graph in a single query, only the root and first level are initially displayed. Node expansion activates asynchronous loading of descendants, optimizing processing times and enabling management of large-scale datasets.
  • Integrated search: Operates directly on the tree structure, filtering and reorganizing the hierarchy to highlight pertinent nodes within their archival context, without generating separate result lists.
  • Reversible and combinable filters: Enable temporary hiding of nodes not satisfying certain conditions (e.g., materials anonymized for privacy), while maintaining possibility of quantitative analysis via SPARQL.
  • Recursive sorting: Enables reorganization of visualization according to user-defined criteria (title, date, IRI, number of descendant elements), applied across all hierarchy levels.
  • Related nodes system: Mouse-over on each node reveals a menu proposing configurable correlation criteria (same creation date, same software, same hash, functional/thematic correlations). Related nodes are automatically highlighted in the view, enabling recognition of transversal connections crossing archival organization.

Archive Visualization

The interface adopts the file system as a metaphor for representing archival materials. The hierarchical structure identifies at the highest level a general grouping of born-digital materials subdivided by media: main computer, external hard drive, and floppy disks. Each grouping replicates the organization of folders and subfolders.

Detail Sheets Display

  • For main groupings: general overview, content description, number of elements, total dimensions, chronological extremes, archival history of materials
  • For folders and files: preview description generated via LLM, integrity information (hash and calculation provenance), extracted technical metadata, references to Evangelisti's works

All details regarding extraction modalities, software utilized, and operation supervision are accessible through dedicated information boxes.

Future Developments

Planned developments include:

  • Advanced search module with combination of multiple criteria
  • Result visualization in list format alternative to tree view
  • Simultaneous management of multiple detail windows for comparison operations
  • Completion of dataset curation
  • Inclusion of analog materials from the collection

ResearchSpace, even in its initial experimental phase, has proven particularly suitable for presenting and valorizing this information, offering a standard base for data management through a SPARQL endpoint based on Blazegraph and the possibility of customizing interfaces according to specific project needs.

This quadripartite methodology enables management of born-digital authorial complexity through an integrated approach that begins with phenomenological study of materials, proceeds through ontological formalization, relies on consolidated technological standards for data management, and concludes with user interfaces that translate semantic complexity into comprehensible access forms—even for non-specialist users—without losing conceptual coherence and descriptive granularity.

The Valerio Evangelisti Archive is a research project dedicated to the preservation and valorization of the literary heritage of one of Italy's most important science fiction and historical fiction authors.
